The Build Process

Every project moves through four phases

Skip a phase and you pay for it later. Here is what each stage actually involves and where homeowners lose the most money.

Weeks 1 - 4

Plan + Permit

Define scope, finalize drawings, gather bids and pull permits before a hammer swings.

  • Written scope of work
  • Three comparable bids
  • Permit applications filed
  • Contingency budget set
Weeks 4 - 8

Mobilize + Demo

Contract signed, deposit released, site protected and demolition handled with proper disposal.

  • Signed contract and schedule
  • Site protection installed
  • Demo and haul-away
  • Discovery review meeting
Weeks 6 - 16

Rough-In + Inspect

Framing, plumbing, electrical and HVAC roughed in. Inspections passed before walls close up.

  • Mechanical rough-in
  • Insulation and vapor barrier
  • Mid-build inspections
  • Drywall and prime coat
Weeks 14 - 22

Finish + Sign-Off

Finish carpentry, paint, tile, fixtures, punch list and retention release with final inspection.

  • Finishes and fixtures
  • Punch list walk-through
  • Final inspection passed
  • Warranty docs and lien waivers
Budget Reality

Where the money actually goes

Most homeowners size the kitchen and forget the rest. A typical mid-range renovation breaks down roughly like this - know it before you sign anything.

Labor35%

Skilled trades, supervision and crew time. The first place lowball bids cut corners.

Materials + Finishes30%

Cabinets, flooring, tile, fixtures, paint. Allowances are where budgets quietly blow up.

Structural + Systems15%

Framing changes, plumbing, electrical, HVAC. Often discovered, never quoted properly upfront.

Permits + Fees5%

Permit pulls, inspections, design fees and dump fees. Small line, real money.

Contingency15%

Reserve for the surprises every renovation generates. Older homes need closer to 20%.

Common Pitfalls

The six mistakes that cost homeowners the most

After thousands of project reviews, the same expensive errors keep showing up. Here is what to do instead.

Picking the lowest bid

The cheapest quote almost always wins by leaving things out. Mid-project change orders make up the difference plus a premium.

Do thisGet three bids and throw out the lowest unless you can explain - line by line - why it is lower.

Skipping permits

Unpermitted work hurts at resale, voids insurance after a fire and turns into a six-figure problem during refinancing.

Do thisPull every permit the code requires. Period. Treat any contractor who suggests otherwise as disqualified.

No contingency budget

Older homes always hide something. Hitting 100% of budget on day one means change orders go on a credit card at 22%.

Do thisHold back 15 to 20% of total budget in cash, untouched, until final walk-through.

Vague allowances

A $3,000 "tile allowance" for an entire bathroom is fiction. Real tile, install and underlayment costs three times that.

Do thisSelect finishes before signing, or require itemized allowances pinned to real product SKUs.

Front-loaded payments

Big deposits give a contractor zero reason to finish on time. Every horror story starts with a 40% up-front check.

Do thisCap deposits at 10%. Tie remaining payments to inspected milestones, hold final 10% for punch list.

No written schedule

Without a milestone schedule, a six-week kitchen becomes a six-month occupation of your house.

Do thisRequire a written schedule with start, milestones and substantial completion dates. Tie liquidated damages to it.
