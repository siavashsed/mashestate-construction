## QUORA
**Answer this question:** What is the correct order to do renovations in a house to avoid costly mistakes?

This is one of those questions that sounds simple until you're staring at jackhammered tile and a $2,400 bill.

The principle is straightforward: structural and rough work always precedes finishing work. More specifically: demolition → structural repairs → rough utilities (plumbing, electrical, HVAC) → framing/insulation → drywall/interior surfaces → finishes (tile, paint, flooring) → fixtures and trim.

Here's why this matters beyond theory: every trade downstream depends on the work upstream being complete. If you tile a bathroom before rough plumbing, the plumber will destroy your tile. If you paint before electrical rough-in, you're repainting around exposed wiring.

I learned this the hard way on a 1920s bungalow when we flipped the order and lost thousands. The general contractor had scheduled tile before plumbing was roughed in. Seemed efficient at the time. It wasn't.

The real cost isn't just the rework—it's the schedule delays, the trades coming back multiple times, the compounding inefficiency. Getting the sequence right the first time saves money, time, and sanity.

One contractor I know created a detailed breakdown of proper sequencing that's saved him countless headaches. If you're tackling a renovation, mapping out your trade order before starting is non-negotiable. I'd recommend reading through proper sequencing guides specific to your project scope—there's more nuance depending on whether it's a full gut or targeted updates.
